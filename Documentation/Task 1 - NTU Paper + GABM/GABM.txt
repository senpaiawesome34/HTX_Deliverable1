GABM (Generative Agent Based Modelling)

What is it?
A GABM combines:
- Agent-Based Modelling (ABM) - many individual agents interact in an environment
- Generative AI (LLM) - generated richer, human-like behaviour rules instead of hardcoded simple logic
** in essence, it's "Physics + AI behaviour"

How it works in the NTU paper:
1. Environment + movement engine - PysocialForce handles motion (speed, repulsion, obstacles,
exit flow)
2. Generate behaviour knowledge
    - Llama 3, via Groq API, is prompted with agent profile + context, e.g. age, gender, Big 5 Traits,
    room fullness, distance to exit
    - Output is converted to a structured behavioural grid (behaviour_grid.json),
    e.g. Impatience State = Yes/No
3. Simulation-time integration
    - Each agent is initialised with profile traits
    - During simulation, behaviour rules from the grid are looked up & used by the behavioural simulator
    variant to alter dynamics (e.g. impatience related behaviour)
4. Compare against baseline
    - Baseline model: Standard social force evacuation (assumes everyone is 'equal', i.e. same Social Forces
    acting on all entities)
    - GABM Model: Social Force + LLM-derived behaviour model
    - Compare evacuation curves, trajectories, congestion patterns

Core Findings:
- Reported result: behaviour-aware model can evacuate faster in early phase (reported ~167% higher initial 
evacuation rate), but may increase bottleneck congestion near exits.
- Key message: more realistic behaviour introduces trade-offs, not just “faster is better”.
- Captures heterogeneous human responses.
- Produces more realistic emergent crowd behaviour.
- Useful for “what-if” emergency planning beyond fixed-rule ABM.


GABM (Non-specific, reserach summary):
According to recent research in the field, GABM addresses the "rationality" gap in traditional models, 
where agents often behave as perfectly logical actors.

Key Insights
- Behavioral Nuance: Traditional models often fail to capture the unpredictable nature of human responses 
in stressful environments like emergency evacuations. LLMs, trained on vast datasets encompassing 
anthropology and psychology, allow agents to exhibit human-like reasoning, judgment, and emotional responses.
- Computational Efficiency: A major challenge in GABM is that calling an LLM for every agent at every time 
step is computationally expensive. Leading research suggests using a "Behavioural Grid"—pre-computing agent 
responses for various conditions (e.g., personality, room fullness, distance to exit) and loading them 
as a lookup table during the simulation.
- Personality Integration: By incorporating the Five-Factor Model (Big Five), agents can be initialized with 
specific traits like high Neuroticism or low Agreeableness. Research shows these traits are primary 
determinants of behavior; e.g. high neuroticism often leads to increased impatience during bottlenecks.

Performance impacts of GABM
- Evacuation Rates: Personality-driven "impatience model" achieved a 167% higher initial evacuation rate 
than a standard baseline (this is the NTU Paper; already mentioned before)
- Group Dynamics: GABM can simulate social cohesion, where agents prioritize staying with their group
(e.g., family or friends) rather than just reaching the exit. While this can slow down initial movement,
group-cohesion models often complete full evacuations faster overall than pure impatience models.
- Predictive Factors: Using machine learning (like Random Forest) to analyze LLM-generated behaviors 
reveals that Neuroticism (32.4% influence) and Agreeableness (19.9% influence) are the strongest 
predictors of whether an agent will become "impatient" in a crowd.

Addressing the 'Black Box' Problem
It is often unclear why an agent chooses a specific action (black box nature of LLMs).
To address this, researchers:
1. Use Random Forest to reverse engineer the LLM's logic
2. Compare spatial distributions of agents at specific time steps to see if "clusters" of certain 
personalities form in ways that match real-world observations.


References:
1. Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior (Park et al., 2023)
Seminal paper demonstrating how LLMs can power autonomous agents in a social environment.
Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442

2. Social Force Model for Pedestrian Dynamics (Helbing & Molnár, 1995)
Foundational physics-based model that most modern evacuation GABMs are built upon.
Link: https://journals.aps.org/pre/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevE.51.4282

3. The "Faster-is-Slower" Effect in Crowds (Helbing et al., 2000)
Explains the physical bottlenecks that occur when agents are too "impatient" - a key concept GABM 
aims to refine.
Link: https://www.nature.com/articles/35035023
